Did physicists finally open a wormhole?

(This is my writing exercise in a popular science communication course)

Our ancestors would never have thought we could travel anywhere in the city riding a giant underground worm, or a train in modern language, as we would ever wonder whether there will come a time when you could teleport to your vacation house on Mars and be back to Earth for dinner. Spreading through headlines the past month however, had been a study1^11﻿ published in 2022 led by Daniel Jafferis from Harvard University claiming to have opened a wormhole with the help of Google’s state-of-the-art quantum computer Sycamore1^11﻿.

Before you start panicking would alien warships start coming through, or would this wormhole disturb the ecology surrounding the lab, I assure you, this wormhole is one more akin to a simulation. But what even is a wormhole? And why do we need a quantum computer to simulate it? For that, we would first need to turn to Einstein’s general theory of relativity and the new paradigm of quantum mechanics, i.e. the theory of big things and theory of small things, and listen to what they have to say about teleportation.

Two types of teleportation

Yes, teleportation is allowed. In fact, it is fairly common among small particles. The two theories each described one way of doing it - through a wormhole or through entangled particles2^22﻿. Wormholes are tunnels connecting to disparate regions of space, allowing objects to take shortcuts and get to places quicker than even light2^22﻿. Entangled particles of the quantum realm on the other hand, exchanges information instantly regardless of how far they are separated, which can be thought of as a teleportation of information2^22﻿.

Are wormholes made of entangled particles?

Here, the creative readers would suggest that entangled particles are connected by a tiny wormhole and communicate by whispering into this tunnel. In fact, this is exactly what the ER=EPR hypothesis is about, whose weird name is made up of the initials of authors of two papers2^22﻿, and basically reads “wormholes = entangled particle pairs” to physicists. Wormholes of our world are holograms cast by entangled particles living on another flatter surface2^22﻿, like how those 3-D holograms in Star Wars emerge from futuristic projectors, and here is where quantum computers enter our discussion.

Controlling the wormhole

Quantum particles are like shy people - they can get a lot of things done simultaneously as long as you don't disturb them. A quantum computer provides a really insulated space for the quantum particles to run through numerous computations simultaneously3^33﻿. That's why one would need a quantum computer to keep up with the system of quantum particles responsible for projecting the wormhole3^33﻿. Yet, even with this the fleet proves to too complicated to pull off. So the researchers simplified a well-established particle theory that has a description of gravity1^11﻿.

The equivalence allows the researchers to custom-make a holographic wormhole by tuning a corresponding quantum system living in Google’s quantum computer. They then successfully past a “bit” of information through it using a teleportation protocol Daniel had previously devised4^44﻿.

Through the wormhole, or blender

I am sure by now some readers would have grown curious of how going through a wormhole was like for that bit of information. It is unfortunately much more gruesome than sci-fi movies would be comfortable depicting. As the state of the quantum system rotates, the bit of information is spread throughout the entire system, before recombining at the opposite end1^11﻿. To make it more explicit, it is like putting a banana into a blender, and an identical banana “un-blends” itself in a connected blender. This is destined to spark intense ethical debates even were wormhole technology to make interstellar travel for humans possible one day.

Even though the wormhole is built in a simplified model of particles, it is the first time in history that humanity came across a tool for us to study wormholes, and we will eventually, by learning from this sandbox, build our own wormholes.
