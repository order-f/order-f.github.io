Variant of Sylow first theorem

One of the conceptual holes that make group theory non-trivial and rich is that the converse of Lagrange‚Äôs theorem is false.

I think of the crux of a group as the invertibility of its elements - and not its associativity. This sentiment is not unjustified. Associativity is embodied by more general objects called categories. Invertibility is by contrast a much rarer condition and is that which really gives a group its structure.

This result follows immediately once we have invertibility. Imagine orbiting a subgroup around. This can never generate a smaller coset, otherwise, any group element defining that group action would be non-invertible. All that‚Äôs left is the realization that that closure condition defining subgroups forces any overlapping cosets to overlap entirely, resulting in the remaining cosets being disjoint.

Is the converse true?

The converse is, however, not true. That is, not all factors of the order of a group correspond to a subgroup.
