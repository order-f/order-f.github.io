Variant of Sylow first theorem

One of the conceptual holes that make group theory non-trivial and rich is that the converse of Lagrange’s theorem is false.

I think of the crux of a group as the invertibility of its elements - and not its associativity. This sentiment is not unjustified. Associativity is embodied by more general objects called categories. Invertibility is by contrast a much rarer condition and is that which really gives a group its structure.

Lagrange’s theorem states that the order of a subgroup always divides that of its encompassing group, or symbolically,

This result follows immediately once we have invertibility. Imagine orbiting a subgroup around. This can never generate a smaller coset, otherwise, any group element defining that group action would be non-invertible. All that’s left is the realization that that closure condition defining subgroups forces any overlapping cosets to overlap entirely, resulting in the remaining cosets being disjoint.

Is the converse true?

The converse is, however, not true. That is, not all factors of the order of a group correspond to a subgroup.

Our natural question to ask now becomes: What are some of the strongest partial converses we can get? I said “converses” because there’re different ways we can strengthen the hypothesis. We can even consider weakening the conclusion, but that won’t be so much of a “converse” in the usual intuitive sense, won’t it?

There’re two ways to approach this question, we either constrain GGG﻿ or the subgroup HHH﻿. If we constrain HHH﻿ to only subgroups with prime-power orders, we do get a partial converse, which coincides with the celebrated Sylow first theorem. It is a partial converse of Lagrange’s theorem in that the implication is only supported on prime powers, rather than the full set of natural numbers:

If you find this proof too technical, fear not. For lack of a better name, let’s call this variant of Sylow first theorem the translated Sylow first theorem. On lower resolutions, this variant theorem is obtained from the composition of theorems on the two ends of Sylow first theorem. Extras

Neat as this translated Sylow first theorem is, it is however not the most general possible partial converse of Lagrange’s theorem. To see this, we stare at the proof. Requiring that GGG﻿ be abelian is really just a way to ensure that all subgroups are normal.
